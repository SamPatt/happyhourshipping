1. Choosing a Platform

There's no single right answer. The most successful vintage sellers use multiple platforms, each serving a different purpose. Here's how they compare:

Square Online

Best for: Stores already using Square POS who want to get online fast.

  • Seamless sync with Square POS — inventory updates within minutes
  • Free plan with unlimited products, pickup, local delivery, basic SEO
  • Paid plans $12–$72/month; processing at 2.9% + $0.30

Limitations: Only 5 free themes. Can only connect Instagram and Facebook — no Etsy, eBay, Depop, or Poshmark integration. Limited SEO tools. Not built for the rich, story-driven product pages that vintage does well with.

Verdict: Good as a quick start or for local pickup/delivery. Not ideal as the primary channel for reaching new customers nationally.

Shopify

Best for: Building a standalone brand with multichannel reach.

  • 80+ themes, massive app ecosystem
  • Integrates with Etsy, eBay, Amazon, TikTok, Instagram, Facebook, Pinterest from one backend
  • Superior SEO and blogging tools — important for vintage where storytelling drives sales
  • Starts at $29/month (no free tier)

Verdict: Best long-term choice for a serious online vintage store. The multi-platform integration is the killer feature.

Etsy

Best for: Discovery — reaching buyers who don't know your store exists.

  • 88+ million active buyers specifically looking for vintage and handmade
  • The "20-year rule" for vintage gives the category credibility
  • $0.20 per listing fee, 6.5% transaction fee, plus payment processing
  • Vintage keyword has a 150% click-through rate on Etsy

Downsides: Etsy's marketplace has been declining — GMS dropped 6.5% in early 2026, active buyers fell 3.4%. High competition, and you don't own the customer relationship. Fees add up: listing + 6.5% transaction + 3% + $0.25 payment processing + 15% if using Etsy Ads.

Verdict: Excellent as a secondary channel for discovery. Not ideal as your only channel.

Depop

Best for: Trendy vintage — Y2K, 90s, streetwear — targeting Gen Z.

  • 30+ million users, heavily Gen Z and Millennial
  • 90% of transactions on mobile — casual, social shopping
  • No listing fees; 10% selling fee
  • Sellers who include short video clips see 42% more saves and 28% higher conversion

Verdict: Worth listing trendy, youth-oriented pieces. Not the right fit for high-end or older vintage (60s–80s).

Poshmark

Best for: Branded vintage and items priced $30+.

  • 80+ million users with an active social community
  • Simplified shipping: prepaid USPS Priority Mail label, flat $7.67 for up to 5 lbs
  • Highest fees: flat $2.95 on sales under $15, 20% commission on sales $15+
  • A $100 dress nets roughly $67 after fees

Verdict: Items under $15 are barely worth listing. Good for mid-to-high price point items only.

The Multi-Platform Strategy

Sellers using crosslisting tools to list across multiple platforms report 2–3x higher sell-through rates without sourcing additional inventory.

Use a crosslisting tool to manage this efficiently:

  • Vendoo ($8.99/month, free tier available) — supports eBay, Poshmark, Depop, Etsy, Shopify
  • List Perfectly ($29–$69/month) — supports 10+ platforms, stronger bulk-listing tools

The most profitable resellers use a tiered approach: list high-value items on their own website (lowest fees), mid-range on Etsy/eBay (marketplace traffic), and trendy pieces on Depop/Poshmark (younger audience, faster turnover).

2. Photography and Listings

90% of online shoppers say image quality is "extremely" or "very" important — it ranks higher than price in purchasing decisions.

How Many Photos

Minimum 4 per item. Ideal set:

  1. Full front view (on model, mannequin, or hanger)
  2. Full back view
  3. Close-up of label/brand tag
  4. Close-up of flaws, unique details, fabric texture, or hardware
  5. Detail of pattern or print (if applicable)
  6. Measurement reference or flat lay

Lighting and Method

  • Natural light near a large window is the gold standard — indirect sunlight avoids harsh shadows
  • Shoot at the same time of day for consistency
  • A smartphone camera is sufficient — focus on lighting over equipment
  • Shoot in square (1:1) format for Depop/Instagram compatibility
  • On a model is best for showing fit and drape — even the store owner works
  • On hangers is fastest; use wooden or velvet, never wire

Measurements Are Mandatory

Vintage sizing is wildly inconsistent with modern sizing. A "Size 12" from 1975 is closer to a modern 6–8. Always provide actual body measurements.

Every listing needs:

  • Chest/bust (pit to pit, doubled)
  • Waist (at natural waist)
  • Hips (at widest point)
  • Length (shoulder to hem for tops/dresses, waist to hem for skirts/pants)
  • Sleeve length (shoulder seam to cuff)
  • Inseam (for pants)
  • Shoulder width (seam to seam)

Condition Grading

Establish a consistent scale and use it on every listing:

GradeDescription
ExcellentNo visible wear, appears unworn
Very GoodMinimal wear, no notable rips/stains/missing parts
GoodSlight signs of age (minor fading, creasing) but maintains integrity
FairNoticeable wear but still wearable — all imperfections noted in detail

Always disclose: rips, stains, discoloration, pilling, missing closures, odors, and alterations. Customers should be pleasantly surprised, not disappointed.

Writing Titles for Search

Especially on Etsy, pack relevant keywords into the title:

"Vintage 1970s Floral Maxi Dress | Boho Prairie Dress | 70s Hippie Festival Dress | Size Small"

Include: era/decade, style, garment type, color, material if notable, size. Use all 13 tags on Etsy with keyword variations and synonyms. Research keywords using Etsy search dropdowns, Marmalead, or eRank.

Efficiency: Batch Processing

Every piece is one-of-a-kind, so every piece needs its own listing. The only way to make this sustainable:

  1. Batch process: Photograph 10–20 items in one session, then write all descriptions together
  2. Template descriptions: Standard format (measurements, condition, shipping, returns) with specifics filled in
  3. Voice-to-text: Dictate descriptions while looking at the item
  4. Crosslisting tools: List once, push to multiple platforms

3. Inventory Management

With one-of-a-kind items, selling something online that just walked out the door in-store is a real and common risk. Every oversell means a canceled order, a disappointed customer, and a potential negative review.

The Sync Gap

Most POS-to-online-store syncs (including Square) update within minutes, not seconds. That lag window is the danger zone during busy in-store periods.

Best Practices

  • Set inventory to 1 for all one-of-a-kind items — sounds obvious, but some sellers leave quantity blank
  • Pull items immediately — when something sells online, remove it from the floor within the hour
  • Segment inventory: Designate some items as "online only" and others as "in-store only" to eliminate overlap entirely
  • Buffer strategy: Keep high-demand items online-only for the first 2 weeks, then move to the floor if they don't sell
  • Daily reconciliation: At end of day, reconcile physical shelf counts against the system to catch items that were moved, tried on, or sold without scanning

Common Pitfalls

  • The "I'll update it later" trap: Someone buys in-store during a rush, the clerk doesn't scan it, the item stays listed online
  • Misplaced items: Customer picks something up, puts it back in the wrong spot — shows available online but can't be found to ship
  • Hold requests: A local customer calls to hold something that's still listed online. Need a consistent policy: either remove from online or time-limit the hold
  • Undisclosed damage: An item gets damaged on the floor but nobody updates the listing

4. Pricing Strategy

The Vintage Pricing Formula

(Total Acquisition Cost) × (Overhead Multiplier) × (1 + Markup%) = Price

Acquisition cost (all-in):

  • Purchase price of the item
  • Repair/restoration costs
  • Cleaning (dry cleaning, hand washing, odor removal)
  • Mileage/travel to acquire
  • Time spent sourcing (valued at an hourly rate)

Listing overhead: Photography time, photo editing, listing creation, platform listing fees.

Markup: 40–60% on combined costs is standard, adjusted for condition, trend value, customer demand, and designer/rarity.

Worked example: A Betsey Johnson dress with $62 in acquisition costs and $22 in listing overhead = $84.20 combined. At 60% markup: $84.20 × 1.60 = $134.72.

Successful vintage stores target 300–500% markup on raw acquisition cost — a $10 thrift store find sells for $30–$50.

Online vs. In-Store

Most successful vintage stores charge more online to cover:

  • Photography and listing labor ($5–$15 per item in time)
  • Shipping materials ($0.25–$1.25 per package)
  • Platform fees (3–20% depending on platform)
  • The wider audience — online buyers pay for convenience and selection

Common approaches:

  • Same price + buyer pays shipping: Most transparent
  • Bake shipping in: Raise price $5–$10, offer "free shipping" — converts better psychologically but looks pricier in search
  • Platform-specific pricing: Charge more on Poshmark (20% fee) than on your own site (2.9% processing)

5. Shipping

Polymailers vs. Boxes

Use polymailers (~$0.25 each) for t-shirts, blouses, pants, skirts, light dresses, scarves — anything soft that folds without damage. Use boxes (~$1.25 each) for beaded/sequined items, structured pieces (hats, shoes, handbags), and multi-item orders. USPS requires poly mailers to be at least 2 mils thick for up to 5 lbs, 4 mils for up to 10 lbs.

Carrier Recommendations

  • Under 1 lb: USPS Ground Advantage — cheapest, typically $3–$5
  • 1–5 lbs: USPS Priority Mail or Ground Advantage — $7–$12 depending on distance
  • Cost-saving tools: Pirate Ship, ShipStation, or Stamps.com provide discounted USPS/UPS rates vs. retail post office prices

Packaging Tips

  • Fold neatly. Tissue paper inside reduces wrinkles and adds a nice touch
  • Include a handwritten thank-you note or business card
  • For delicate vintage, wrap in acid-free tissue to prevent color transfer

Return Policy

Most vintage stores use all sales final with a misrepresentation exception. This is standard and accepted by buyers as long as descriptions and photos are thorough. 81% of shoppers check the return policy before buying, so display it prominently.

Other options: store credit only (within 7–14 days), or selective returns on full-price items. Note that Etsy, Depop, and Poshmark have their own buyer protection policies that override your store policy on those platforms.

6. Marketing and Discovery

Instagram

The most important platform for vintage. 81% of Instagram users research products on the app, and half end up buying.

  • Styled-in-context photos get 30% higher engagement than standard product shots
  • Use Stories for behind-the-scenes content: sourcing trips, new arrivals, styling ideas
  • Reels perform well: try-on videos, "style this 3 ways," era-specific lookbooks
  • User-generated content (customers wearing purchases) increases conversion by 29%
  • Key hashtags: #vintageclothing, #vintagefashion, #thrifted, plus era tags (#70sfashion, #90svintage)

TikTok

Nearly half of Gen Z prefer TikTok and Instagram over Google for product discovery. The resale market is growing 21x faster than traditional fashion retail, and TikTok is a key driver.

  • Content that works: thrift hauls, "what I found today," transformation videos, styling challenges, "guess the era" games
  • Video clips of garments see 42% more saves and 28% higher conversion
  • Micro-influencers (10k–50k followers) see 60% higher engagement than celebrities. ROI: $5.78 for every $1 spent
  • TikTok Shop integration allows direct purchasing from videos

SEO

  • Optimize product titles with era, style, material, color, size
  • Blog content drives organic traffic: "How to date vintage clothing," "Guide to 70s fashion," "How to care for vintage fabrics"
  • Businesses prioritizing educational content generate 67% more leads than promotional-focused competitors
  • Optimize Google Business Profile — 76% of people who do a local search visit a business within 24 hours

Email Marketing

Build an email list from day one. This is the only channel you truly own — platforms can change algorithms, but you always have your list.
  • Use exit-intent popups, in-store signup sheets, "get first access to new arrivals" as incentives
  • Weekly "new arrivals" emails drive traffic and create urgency for one-of-a-kind items
  • Include care tips, styling advice, and vintage history alongside promotion — value beyond just selling

Community

  • In-store events (styling workshops, era-themed parties) that get shared socially
  • Businesses that build communities see 50% higher customer retention
  • Loyalty programs: 79% of consumers say they're more likely to keep buying

7. Real Stories and Lessons

Karen at "Hunting for Vintage" — 12+ years on Etsy

Karen runs Lion & Lamb Vintage on Etsy and blogs transparently about the business. Her insights are some of the most honest writing about vintage selling online.

Quality over quantity: Her colleague Heather cut from 400 listings to 200 curated pieces over three months. The result: a "wonderful boutique feel" with improved sales. Less inventory, better inventory.

Spending discipline: Karen spent ~$2,100 on merchandise in 2024 and plans to cut to under $800, focusing on "older, great quality items or rare quirky things" rather than volume.

Specialization matters: She describes her store as "quite a mish-mosh of stuff" and questions whether lack of focus hurts conversion.

The honest take: Sales declined in Q4 2024 (typically the best quarter). She's called 2025 her "litmus test year" — if sales don't improve, she'll liquidate and close.

A larger vintage operation running both physical stores and e-commerce. Their key innovation: upcycling unsellable inventory through a "Re-Design" studio that turns would-be waste into new revenue. They note that 40–60% of vintage sourcing can be unsellable as-is — having a plan for that inventory matters.

Cross-Platform Seller Insights

From seller communities and case studies:

  • Y2K and 90s vintage sells best on Depop and Poshmark. Older vintage (60s–80s) and designer pieces do better on eBay, Etsy, and specialized platforms. Match inventory to the right audience.
  • Video is increasingly mandatory. Short clips generate significantly more engagement and sales than photos alone.
  • Automation is essential at scale. Larger operations report saving up to $5,000/month in operational costs by automating order processing and customer service.

Market Context

The global secondhand apparel market reached $45.3 billion in 2024, projected to reach $126.6 billion by 2033. The resale market is growing 7x faster than overall retail. 93% of Americans have bought or sold secondhand online.

Vintage specifically benefits from sustainability trends, nostalgia culture driven by social media, and Gen Z's preference for unique over mass-produced fashion.

8. Action Plan

Phase 1 — Get Online Fast (Weeks 1–2)

  1. Enable Square Online (free tier) to create a basic store synced with existing POS
  2. Photograph the 20–30 best/most unique items using natural light and a plain backdrop
  3. Write detailed descriptions with measurements and condition for each
  4. Set up Instagram and start posting styled photos

Phase 2 — Expand Reach (Months 1–2)

  1. Open an Etsy shop and list the same items with Etsy-optimized titles and all 13 tags
  2. Consider Depop for trendy/Y2K/90s pieces
  3. Set up a crosslisting tool (Vendoo free tier)
  4. Start building an email list (signup at the register, on the website, and on social media)

Phase 3 — Optimize and Scale (Month 3+)

  1. Evaluate whether Shopify would better serve as a branded home base
  2. Add TikTok content (thrift hauls, styling videos, new arrivals)
  3. Develop a consistent batch workflow to process 5–10 items per day
  4. Implement end-of-day inventory reconciliation
  5. Analyze which platforms are generating sales and double down
